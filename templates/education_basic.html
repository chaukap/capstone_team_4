Thinking like an attacker

A Simple Example

Balding Bears

For this example, let's imagine that you are a bear researcher that just made a startling discovery- some of our favorite school mascots are going bald! You have no idea what is causing the sudden hair loss but you have a hunch that it has to do with their eating habits. To test your hypothesis, you collect some information about the bears.

Name State Salmon Eaten
Avalanche Pennsylvania 10
Bananas Maine 13
Benny Maryland 14
Boomer Missouri 12
Grizz California 18
Kody Washington 14
Monte Montana 18
Nanook Maine 17
Range New Jersey 16
Scott Highlander California 12

It's not easy to obtain this information. The mascot are worried that if the fact that they are balding goes public it would hurt their reputation. To gain the trust of the bears, you promise to never release their names.

After a week spent searching for the cause, nothing about this information stands out to you. Perhaps some other researchers will have better luck. You decide to publish some statistics about this data to let others weigh in. To protect the privacy of the bears you do not release any identifiable information.

Number of Bears Average Salmon Eaten
10 14.4

In the mean time, you get a call from a Bay Area number. It's none other than Oski, the beloved Cal mascot. Poor Oski is balding and hopes that your research can save his iconic image. It's a well-known fact that Oski is a world-class salmon catcher which could inspire new hypotheses.

You learn the following about Oski:

Name State Salmon Eaten
Oski California 48

Eager to share your new data, you release the same statistics as before to the public:

Number of Bears Average Salmon Eaten
11 17.5454

Attacking the Data

While your fellow researchers get to work looking for a cause, the press decides to run a story about the balding bears rumor. One reporter with some basic statistics knowlege decides to dig deeper into the information you published.

They begin by calculating the total salmon eaten by all of the bears in the dataset using the first dataset you published:

14.4 * 10 = 144

Next, they do the same calculation using the updated dataset.

17.5454 * 11 = 192

They also observe that the size of the two datasets only differ by one bear. That means that the number of salmon eaten by the latest addition to the database must be the difference between the two numbers!

192 - 144 = 48

48 salmon is a lot! The reporter can only think of one mascot that could possibly eat that many salmon: Oski.

Confident that Oski must be in the dataset, the reporter runs a story about poor Oski's hair loss. The mascot is heartbroken! He gave you his information in confidence and now the whole world knows about his affliction.

The Problem with Anonymized Data

As you can see from this example, privacy is more complex than simply removing names from data.

Armed with a little background knowlege, like the fact that Oski is a prolific salmon catcher, an attacker can make educated guesses about the members of a dataset. These educated guesses can de devastating to individuals that give their information in confidence but are outed anyways.

Differential Privacy provides a defense from this type of privacy attack. Follow the link to learn more about differential privacy.
